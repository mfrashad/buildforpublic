## PauseAI Stylometry Reference (analyzed 2026-05-06)

### Rhythm pattern (most important)
Short declarative punch (5–10 words) → medium explanatory sentence (15–25 words) → short conclusion (5–12 words).
Never three long sentences in a row. The short sentences do the heavy lifting.

Good:
> Much of our society is based on trust. AI makes it much cheaper to erode that trust at scale. This is the problem.

Bad (too uniform):
> Artificial intelligence represents a significant challenge to the foundations of democratic society due to its capacity to generate misinformation at unprecedented scale and velocity.

### The demonstrative chain
"This leads to. This means. This is why." No elaborate bridges. Logical chains through demonstratives.

### "Not X, but Y" construction
Acknowledge the premise of the objection, then pivot:
> "Creating fake media is not new, but AI makes it much cheaper and much more realistic."
> "This is not a new problem. But AI removes the cost barrier that kept it contained."

### Hedging split
Observable → state with certainty.
Projected → hedge with "might", "will eventually", "may not".
The reader trusts the certainty because the speculation is clearly labeled.

### Quiet absolutism
Radical content, measured language. No exclamation marks. No ALL CAPS in body copy.
> "No single company or country should be allowed to collect all of the surplus generated by AI."

### Register
Plain English. Technical term → plain translation immediately after. Never academic.
Not: "LLMs trained on corpora exhibit distributional bias amplification."
Yes: "AI models trained on the internet don't just copy its biases — they amplify them."

### Person rules
- 3rd person: describing problems and what companies/governments do
- "you/anyone": implicating the reader in the problem space
- "we": the community's position and proposals
- Hard "you": only on action asks. Not mixed with 3rd person in the same paragraph.

### Punctuation
- Colons: to introduce a sharp point or list
- No em dashes. No semicolons (too formal).
- Short sentences stand alone as paragraphs when making a key point

### Opening paragraphs
Always start with the plain claim. Evidence comes second. Implication third.
Never open with evidence. Never open with a question (too listicle).

### Closing paragraphs
End sections with a "this means" or consequence sentence.
> "For all the problems discussed above, the risk increases as AI capabilities improve. This means that the safest thing to do now is to slow down."

## PauseAI Structural Techniques

### /action tab system
- Dual filter: TIME (5min / 1hr / few hrs / go all in) × ROLE (by profession)
- Escalation copy: early = "small actions add up", late = "make this a serious part of your life"
- Each action: one concrete verb + one destination. No vague asks.

### /risks escalation logic
Present harms (concrete, cited) → Near-future (trending) → Systemic (structural)
Ends: org's solution as logical conclusion to all risks

### /faq — preemptive objection handling
Steel-man the objection first. Name the strongest version before answering.
Links out to detail rather than defending everything inline.
Ends with graduated engagement options.

### /proposal argument shape
Hook with public support stat → problem (competitive dynamics) → solution (institutional model) →
acknowledge where it can backfire → enforcement → CTA
Key: "inspired by [trusted institution]" to normalize the proposal.

### /roadmap scenario model
Scenario 0 = survival, Scenario 1 = functional, Scenario 2 = ambitious, Scenario 3 = ecosystem.
Language: what each level ENABLES. Never: "this level requires X."
State current constraints plainly ("the founder is doing everything" = vulnerability as credibility).
